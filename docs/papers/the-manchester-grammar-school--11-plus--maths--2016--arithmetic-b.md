<div class='question question'>

Howard discovers a method to find the heights of buildings. He measures the distance to the foot of the building, $d \ \text{metres}$. 
Then he measures the angle to the horizontal when he looks up at the top of the building, as shown in the diagram.

![missing image](/papers/missing_image.svg)

Using that angle, he then finds the quantity called the **tannangle** from the table below:

| $\text{Angle} \degree$ | $10$  | $20$  | $30$  | $40$  | $50$  | $60$  | $70$  | $80$  |
|------------------------|-------|-------|-------|-------|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| $\text{Tannangle}$     | $0.2$ | $0.4$ | $0.6$ | $0.8$ | $1.2$ | $1.7$ | $2.7$ | $5.7$ |

The height of the building, $h \ \text{metres}$, is given by the following calculation:

$h = d \times \text{tannangle}$

e.g. if the building is $50 \ \text{m}$ away and the angle is $30\degree$ then the height is given by:

$h = 50 \times 0.6 = 30 \ \text{m}$

The stopping distance for a car is made up of two parts. 

The first is the distance travelled by the car while the driver is reacting to something that makes them want to brake. 
This is called the $\textbf{Thinking Distance}$. 

The second is the distance travelled by the car while the brakes are applied. This is called the $\textbf{Braking Distance}$. 

The $\textbf{Stopping Distance}$ is given by adding these two distances together.

So $\textbf{Stopping Distance} = \textbf{Thinking Distance} + \textbf{Braking Distance}$
